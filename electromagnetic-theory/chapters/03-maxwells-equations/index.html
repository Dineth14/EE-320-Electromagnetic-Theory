Chapter 3 — Maxwell's Equations

The complete, unified framework of classical electromagnetism

3.1 The Four Maxwell Equations

Maxwell's equations are the foundation of all classical electromagnetic phenomena. In their most general form:

Gauss (E) $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \qquad \oint\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Physical meaning: Electric charges are sources of electric field lines. Positive charges emit field lines; negative charges absorb them.

Gauss (B) $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0 \qquad \oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$$

Physical meaning: No magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always close on themselves.

Faraday $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \qquad \oint\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

Physical meaning: A changing magnetic field induces an electric field (electromagnetic induction).

Ampère–Maxwell $$\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \qquad \oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$$

Physical meaning: Magnetic fields are produced by electric currents and by changing electric fields (displacement current).

3.2 Faraday's Law — Full Treatment

Michael Faraday discovered (1831) that a changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an electromotive force (EMF):

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

Lenz's Law

The induced current flows in a direction to oppose the change in flux that produced it (the negative sign).

Motional EMF

For a conductor moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a magnetic field:

$$\mathcal{E} = \int(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

Transformer EMF

For a stationary loop in a time-varying $\mathbf{B}$:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\int_S\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$$
1

Start with $\oint\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$

2

Apply Stokes' theorem to the left side: $\int_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{E})\cdot d\mathbf{A} = -\int_S\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$

3

Since this holds for any surface $S$:

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

3.3 Displacement Current

Maxwell realized that Ampère's law ($\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$) was incomplete — it violated charge conservation for time-varying fields.

1

Taking the divergence of Ampère's law: $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}) = \mu_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}$. The LHS is identically zero, but the continuity equation requires $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} \neq 0$ in general.

2

Using Gauss's law: $\rho = \varepsilon_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}$, so $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} + \varepsilon_0\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) = 0$, i.e., $\nabla\cdot\left(\mathbf{J} + \varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right) = 0$.

3

Maxwell's fix: replace $\mathbf{J}$ with $\mathbf{J} + \varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$. The term $\mathbf{J}_d = \varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$ is the displacement current density.

The physical consequence: a time-varying electric field produces a magnetic field, even in the absence of conduction current. This completes the symmetry between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ and predicts electromagnetic waves.

3.4 Maxwell's Equations in Matter

Using auxiliary fields $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$ and $\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M}$:

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} = \rho_f \qquad \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$$ $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \qquad \nabla\times\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}_f + \frac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t}$$

For linear, isotropic media: $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}$, $\mathbf{J}_f = \sigma\mathbf{E}$.

3.5 The Electromagnetic Wave Equation

1

In free space ($\rho = 0$, $\mathbf{J} = 0$), take the curl of Faraday's law:

$$\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\times\mathbf{B})$$
2

Use vector identity: $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E}$. Since $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$:

$$-\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\times\mathbf{B})$$
3

Substitute Ampère-Maxwell ($\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$):

$$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$
4

This is a wave equation with phase velocity:

$$v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}} = c \approx 3\times10^8 \text{ m/s}$$
Eq. 3.1 $$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu\varepsilon\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

An identical equation holds for $\mathbf{B}$. Light is an electromagnetic wave — one of the most profound discoveries in physics.

3.6 Poynting's Theorem

1

Dot Faraday's law with $\mathbf{H}$ and the Ampère-Maxwell law with $\mathbf{E}$, then subtract:

2

$\mathbf{H}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) - \mathbf{E}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{H}) = -\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H})$

3

After substitution:

$$-\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}) = \mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\frac{1}{2}\varepsilon E^2 + \frac{1}{2}\mu H^2\right)$$

The Poynting vector describes the direction and rate of energy flow:

Eq. 3.2 $$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}$$

Units: W/m². Poynting's theorem in integral form:

$$-\oint_S\mathbf{S}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V\mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{E}\,dV + \frac{d}{dt}\int_V\left(\frac{1}{2}\varepsilon E^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu}B^2\right)dV$$

Power flowing out = Power dissipated + Rate of energy storage change.

3.7 Time-Harmonic (Phasor) Form

For sinusoidal steady-state, replace $\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \to j\omega$. Fields become complex phasors:

$$\nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{E}} = -j\omega\mu\tilde{\mathbf{H}} \qquad \nabla\times\tilde{\mathbf{H}} = (j\omega\varepsilon + \sigma)\tilde{\mathbf{E}}$$

Complex permittivity: $\varepsilon_c = \varepsilon' - j\varepsilon'' = \varepsilon - j\frac{\sigma}{\omega}$. The loss tangent: $\tan\delta = \frac{\varepsilon''}{\varepsilon'} = \frac{\sigma}{\omega\varepsilon}$.

3.8 Boundary Conditions

At the interface between two media, derived from the integral forms:

1. Normal D: Apply Gauss's law for $\mathbf{D}$ to a thin pillbox straddling the interface:

$$D_{1n} - D_{2n} = \rho_s$$

2. Normal B: Apply Gauss's law for $\mathbf{B}$:

$$B_{1n} = B_{2n}$$

3. Tangential E: Apply Faraday's law to a thin loop:

$$E_{1t} = E_{2t}$$

4. Tangential H: Apply Ampère's law:

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times(\mathbf{H}_1 - \mathbf{H}_2) = \mathbf{K}$$

where $\mathbf{K}$ is the surface current density. If $\mathbf{K} = 0$: $H_{1t} = H_{2t}$.
