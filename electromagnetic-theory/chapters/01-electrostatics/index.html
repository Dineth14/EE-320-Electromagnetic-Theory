Chapter 1 — Electrostatics

Charges at rest, the fields they create, and the energy they store

1.1 Coulomb's Law

Charles-Augustin de Coulomb established experimentally (1785) that the force between two point charges varies directly as the product of their magnitudes and inversely as the square of the distance between them.

Eq. 1.1 $$\mathbf{F}_{12} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{12}$$

where $\varepsilon_0 = 8.854\times10^{-12}$ F/m is the permittivity of free space, $q_1, q_2$ are point charges (in coulombs), and $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{12}$ is the unit vector from charge 2 to charge 1.

1

Observation: Coulomb used a torsion balance to measure the force between charged spheres. He found $F \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$: doubling the distance reduced the force by a factor of four.

2

Proportionality to charges: By sharing charge (touching an identical uncharged sphere), he halved $q$ and verified $F \propto q_1 q_2$.

3

Vector form: Forces act along the line joining the two charges. Defining $\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{12} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}$, and introducing the proportionality constant $k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$:

$$\mathbf{F}_{12} = k_e \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}_{12}$$

Superposition Principle

The total force on a charge $q$ due to $N$ other charges is the vector sum of individual Coulomb forces:

Eq. 1.2 $$\mathbf{F} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{q_i}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_i|^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}_i$$

This linearity is fundamental — it means electromagnetic fields obey the principle of superposition, allowing us to decompose complex charge distributions into sums of point charges.

1.2 Electric Field E

Rather than thinking of force at a distance, we define the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ as the force per unit positive test charge:

Eq. 1.3 $$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \lim_{q_0\to 0}\frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

Electric Field Lines

Field lines are curves whose tangent at every point is parallel to $\mathbf{E}$. Properties:

  • Lines begin on positive charges and end on negative charges
  • The density of lines is proportional to $|\mathbf{E}|$
  • Field lines never cross (the field is single-valued)

Electric Flux

The electric flux through a surface $S$ is:

$$\Phi_E = \int_S \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A}$$

For a closed surface, this becomes the left side of Gauss's law.

1.3 Gauss's Law

Gauss's law relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge:

Eq. 1.4 $$\oint_S \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}$$
1

Consider a single point charge $q$ at the origin. Its field is $\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$.

2

Place a sphere of radius $R$ centred on the charge. On this surface, $\mathbf{E}$ is radial and constant in magnitude:

$$\oint \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = E(R)\cdot 4\pi R^2 = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2}\cdot 4\pi R^2 = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$$
3

By the divergence theorem, this generalizes to any closed surface and, by superposition, to any charge distribution. The result is independent of the surface shape.

4

Differential form: Applying the divergence theorem $\oint \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = \int_V \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}\,dV$ and using $Q_{\text{enc}} = \int_V \rho\,dV$:

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Applications of Gauss's Law

For a sphere of total charge $Q$ and radius $a$:

Outside ($r > a$): By spherical symmetry, draw a Gaussian sphere of radius $r$:

$$E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0} \implies \mathbf{E} = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

Inside ($r < a$): Only charge within radius $r$ contributes. For uniform charge density $\rho = \frac{3Q}{4\pi a^3}$:

$$E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{\rho \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3}{\varepsilon_0} \implies \mathbf{E} = \frac{Qr}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 a^3}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius $s$ and length $L$ coaxial with the line charge. By symmetry, $\mathbf{E}$ is radial and constant on this surface:

$$E(2\pi s L) = \frac{\lambda L}{\varepsilon_0} \implies \mathbf{E} = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 s}\hat{\mathbf{s}}$$

Choose a pillbox Gaussian surface. By symmetry, $\mathbf{E}$ is perpendicular to the sheet and equal on both flat faces:

$$2EA = \frac{\sigma A}{\varepsilon_0} \implies \mathbf{E} = \frac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

1.4 Electric Potential

Since $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = 0$ in electrostatics, $\mathbf{E}$ is conservative, and we can define a scalar potential:

Eq. 1.5 $$V(\mathbf{r}) = -\int_{\mathcal{O}}^{\mathbf{r}} \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

where $\mathcal{O}$ is a reference point (usually infinity). The relation between $V$ and $\mathbf{E}$ is:

$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$$

Potential of a Point Charge

$$V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q}{r}$$

Work and Energy

The work done in moving a charge $q_0$ from $\mathbf{a}$ to $\mathbf{b}$ is:

$$W = -q_0\int_{\mathbf{a}}^{\mathbf{b}}\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = q_0[V(\mathbf{b}) - V(\mathbf{a})]$$

This is path-independent — a hallmark of conservative fields.

Equipotential Surfaces

Surfaces of constant $V$. They are always perpendicular to field lines. No work is done moving a charge along an equipotential.

1.5 Poisson's and Laplace's Equations

1

Start from Gauss's law in differential form: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$

2

Substitute $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$:

$$\nabla\cdot(-\nabla V) = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$
3

This gives Poisson's equation:

Eq. 1.6 $$\nabla^2 V = -\frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$

In charge-free regions ($\rho = 0$), this reduces to Laplace's equation:

Eq. 1.7 $$\nabla^2 V = 0$$

Solutions are determined uniquely by boundary conditions (Dirichlet: $V$ specified; Neumann: $\frac{\partial V}{\partial n}$ specified).

1.6 Energy Stored in Electric Fields

1

The energy to assemble $N$ point charges from infinity is:

$$W = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N}q_i V(\mathbf{r}_i)$$
2

For continuous distributions, $W = \frac{1}{2}\int \rho V\,d\tau$. Using $\rho = \varepsilon_0 \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}$ and integration by parts:

$$W = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{2}\int |\mathbf{E}|^2\,d\tau$$
3

The energy density (energy per unit volume) is therefore:

Eq. 1.8 $$u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2$$

1.7 Conductors and Dielectrics

Conductors

In electrostatic equilibrium: $\mathbf{E} = 0$ inside, charge resides on the surface, surface field is $\mathbf{E} = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

Dielectrics and Polarization

In a dielectric, an applied field induces dipole moments. The polarization $\mathbf{P}$ (dipole moment per unit volume) gives rise to bound charges:

$$\rho_b = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{P}, \qquad \sigma_b = \mathbf{P}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

Displacement Field D

To handle free charges separately, we define:

$$\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon_0\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$$

For linear dielectrics, $\mathbf{P} = \varepsilon_0\chi_e\mathbf{E}$, so $\mathbf{D} = \varepsilon\mathbf{E}$ where $\varepsilon = \varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r = \varepsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)$.

Gauss's law for $\mathbf{D}$: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} = \rho_f$ (free charge only).

1.8 Capacitance

Capacitance is defined as $C = Q/V$ for a system storing charge $Q$ at potential difference $V$.

1

Two plates of area $A$ separated by $d$. Using Gauss's law: $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0 = Q/(\varepsilon_0 A)$.

2

Voltage: $V = Ed = Qd/(\varepsilon_0 A)$.

3

Therefore:

$$C = \frac{Q}{V} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$$

Inner radius $a$, outer radius $b$, charge $Q$ on inner shell:

$$E = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}, \qquad V = \frac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\left(\frac{1}{a} - \frac{1}{b}\right)$$ $$C = \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0 ab}{b - a}$$

Inner radius $a$, outer radius $b$, length $L$:

$$E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 s}, \qquad V = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0}\ln\frac{b}{a}$$ $$C = \frac{2\pi\varepsilon_0 L}{\ln(b/a)}$$

The energy stored in a capacitor is $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C}$.
