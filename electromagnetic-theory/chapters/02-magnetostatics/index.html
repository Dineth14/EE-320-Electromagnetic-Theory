Chapter 2 — Magnetostatics

Steady currents, the magnetic fields they produce, and magnetic energy

2.1 Magnetic Force on Moving Charges

A charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ experiences the Lorentz force:

Eq. 2.1 $$\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$$

This force is always perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$, meaning it does no work (only changes direction, not speed).

Cyclotron Motion

1

For a particle moving perpendicular to a uniform $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, the magnetic force provides centripetal acceleration:

$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
2

Solving for the cyclotron radius:

$$r_c = \frac{mv}{qB}$$
3

The cyclotron frequency (independent of speed):

$$\omega_c = \frac{qB}{m}, \qquad f_c = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}$$

If there is also a component of velocity along $\mathbf{B}$, the particle traces a helix.

2.2 Biot-Savart Law

The magnetic field produced by a steady current element $Id\mathbf{l}$ at a point $\mathbf{r}$ is:

Eq. 2.2 $$d\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{I\,d\mathbf{l}\times\hat{\mathbf{r}}'}{|\mathbf{r}'|^2}$$

where $\mu_0 = 4\pi\times10^{-7}$ T·m/A is the permeability of free space.

1

Place wire along $z$-axis carrying current $I$. Find $\mathbf{B}$ at distance $s$ from wire.

2

By symmetry and integration: $d\mathbf{l} = dz\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{r}}' = \frac{s\hat{\mathbf{s}} - z\hat{\mathbf{z}}}{\sqrt{s^2 + z^2}}$

3

After integration over $z \in (-\infty, \infty)$:

$$\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

For a circular loop of radius $R$ carrying current $I$, the field on the axis at distance $z$:

$$B_z = \frac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(R^2 + z^2)^{3/2}}$$

At the center ($z = 0$): $B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$.

An ideal solenoid with $n$ turns per unit length. Inside: $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 n I\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ (uniform). Outside: $\mathbf{B} \approx 0$.

2.3 Ampère's Law

Eq. 2.3 $$\oint \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$$

In differential form (using Stokes' theorem):

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$$

Ampère's law is most useful when symmetry allows $\mathbf{B}$ to be factored out of the integral (infinite wire, solenoid, toroid).

2.4 Gauss's Law for Magnetism

Eq. 2.4 $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0 \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0$$

There are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops. The net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero.

2.5 Vector Magnetic Potential A

Since $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$, we can write $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ for some vector potential $\mathbf{A}$.

1

From $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$ and $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$:

$$\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$$
2

Using vector identity: $\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{A} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$.

3

In the Coulomb gauge ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} = 0$):

$$\nabla^2\mathbf{A} = -\mu_0\mathbf{J}$$

This is a vector Poisson equation, with solution:

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d\tau'$$

2.6 Magnetic Dipole

The magnetic dipole moment of a current loop of area $A$ carrying current $I$ is:

$$\mathbf{m} = I\mathbf{A} = IA\hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

Torque on a Dipole

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{m}\times\mathbf{B}$$

Potential Energy

$$U = -\mathbf{m}\cdot\mathbf{B}$$

Dipole Field (far field)

At large distances from a magnetic dipole:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\text{dip}} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r^3}\left[3(\mathbf{m}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}})\hat{\mathbf{r}} - \mathbf{m}\right]$$

This has the same form as the electric dipole field — both fall off as $1/r^3$.

2.7 Magnetic Materials

In magnetic materials, atomic magnetic moments produce a magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ (magnetic moment per unit volume).

The auxiliary field $\mathbf{H}$:

$$\mathbf{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathbf{B} - \mathbf{M}$$

For linear media: $\mathbf{M} = \chi_m\mathbf{H}$, giving $\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}$ where $\mu = \mu_0(1 + \chi_m) = \mu_0\mu_r$.

Types of Magnetism

  • Diamagnetic ($\chi_m < 0$, small): opposes applied field (e.g., copper, bismuth)
  • Paramagnetic ($\chi_m > 0$, small): aligns with applied field (e.g., aluminum, platinum)
  • Ferromagnetic ($\chi_m \gg 1$): strong alignment, hysteresis (e.g., iron, nickel, cobalt)

Hysteresis

In ferromagnets, $\mathbf{B}$ vs $\mathbf{H}$ traces a hysteresis loop due to domain structure. Key parameters: saturation $B_s$, remanence $B_r$, coercivity $H_c$.

2.8 Inductance

Self-Inductance

The flux through a circuit due to its own current $I$: $\Phi = LI$, where $L$ is the self-inductance.

1

Inside: $B = \mu_0 nI$. Flux through one turn: $\Phi_1 = BA = \mu_0 nIA$.

2

Total flux linkage for $N = nl$ turns: $\Phi = N\Phi_1 = \mu_0 n^2 l A I$.

3
$$L = \frac{\Phi}{I} = \mu_0 n^2 l A = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l}$$

Mutual Inductance

The flux through loop 2 due to current $I_1$ in loop 1: $\Phi_{21} = M_{21}I_1$. The Neumann formula gives $M_{12} = M_{21} = M$.

Magnetic Energy

Eq. 2.5 $$U = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int B^2\,d\tau$$

Energy density in the magnetic field: $u_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}B^2$.
